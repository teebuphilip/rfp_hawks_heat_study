Layer Stack

The goal is to keep the player valuation layer separate from the team aggregation layer so the model stays readable to basketball operations users.

Read In Order

1. DBB2 raw projection

Player output starts in the projection engine. Age, usage, durability, and similar inputs are already baked in.

2. Teammate-neutral normalization

Adjust the player feed so a player is not penalized or inflated by surrounding roster quality.

3. FMV pricing

Apply basketball-ops weights and a cap-constrained market simulation to produce salary-equivalent value.

4. FMVW bridge

Convert team-level FMV into a wins-equivalent number by comparing roster value to historical team wins, with replacement-level and cap context baked in. This is a bridge from market value to expected season wins, not a claim that FMV itself is wins.

What The Win Bridge Means

Definition

The win bridge turns a roster's market value into an expected win total by mapping team FMV totals to actual season wins. It starts from teammate-neutral player value, rolls that into a team-level market value, and then uses historical team outcomes to place that value on a wins scale. In plain English: if a better roster clears for more in the market, it should also project to win more games once the roster is scaled to an NBA season.

How it is used

Use it for team comparisons, roster construction, and player-to-roster context. It is the step that lets the user ask, "How much does this player or roster shape move wins?" without pretending the market-value layer is already a win model. The bridge is intentionally built to be readable at the front-office level: strong rosters, weak rosters, replacement-level slots, and the effect of cap pressure are all part of the translation.

What it is not

It is not a direct box-score-to-wins formula, not a fantasy scoring shortcut, and not a replacement for teammate-neutral player valuation. It is the translation layer that comes after FMV.

Why This Is Different

Compared with VORP / Win Shares

Those approaches are mainly trying to attribute team wins back to players. DBB2 starts one layer earlier: it neutralizes teammate context first, then prices the player in a market. The output is not just a win-share estimate. It is market value on top of a cleaner player signal.

Compared with BPM / RAPTOR / LEBRON / EPM-style models

Those systems generally aim to estimate player impact directly from a mix of box score and plus-minus style inputs. DBB2 is narrower at the start: it keeps the player layer teammate-neutral, then turns that into fair market value. That makes it easier to talk about value, overpay, and roster construction without confusing the player signal with the team signal.

Compared with a fantasy auction model

This is not built to maximize category stats or win a fantasy draft. It is built to price an NBA player inside a cap-constrained market, then use that valuation as the basis for a wins bridge or team comparison when needed.

How To Read It

Player-first

If you are asking who is better between two players, look at the FMVW View first. That is where the teammate-neutral valuation is the point, not the side effect.

Team context second

If you are asking how a roster shape behaves over a season, use the team layers and the league table. That is where core concentration, depth, interaction, and pressure begin to matter.

Wins bridge is derived

The wins-equivalent number is useful, but it is downstream of FMV. It is there to help the conversation, not replace the player value signal.
